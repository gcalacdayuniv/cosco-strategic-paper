# Chapter 6: Strategy Analysis and Choice

## A. Introduction
The Strategy Analysis and Choice stage is a critical phase in the strategic-management process where an organization, such as Cosco Integrated Office Systems, Inc., identifies and evaluates alternative courses of action to achieve its long-term objectives and mission. For Cosco, a firm currently navigating a P5.58 million net loss and a cumulative deficit of P29.5 million, this chapter represents the transition from understanding its environment to making decisive moves for survival and growth. Strategic management is not merely a bundle of techniques, but a process of analytical thinking and the commitment of resources to action.

The framework for strategy formulation involves three stages: the Input Stage, the Matching Stage, and the Decision Stage. This analysis focuses on the Input Stage, which summarizes the basic input information needed to formulate strategies through tools like the External Factor Evaluation (EFE) Matrix.

## B. Input Stage
The Input Stage is the foundation of the strategy-formulation framework. It consists of tools that allow strategists to measure subjectively the relative importance of both external and internal factors. For Cosco, the input stage requires gathering data from previous environmental audits - specifically the PESTLE analysis and Porter's Five Forces to populate the matrices that will guide further decision-making.

The primary tools used in this stage include:
1. External Factor Evaluation (EFE) Matrix: Used to summarize and evaluate external economic, social, cultural, demographic, environmental, political, governmental, legal, technological, and competitive information.
2. Internal Factor Evaluation (IFE) Matrix: Used to summarize and evaluate the major strengths and weaknesses in the functional areas of a business.
3. Competitive Profile Matrix (CPM): Identifies a firm's major competitors and its particular strengths and weaknesses in relation to a sample firm's strategic position.

The information derived from these tools provides the necessary raw material for the Matching Stage, where internal strengths and weaknesses are paired with external opportunities and threats to generate feasible alternative strategies.

### External Factor Evaluation (EFE) Matrix
The EFE Matrix is a strategic-management tool used to visualize and prioritize the opportunities and threats that Cosco is facing in the Philippine furniture industry. It quantifies how well the company is currently responding to its environment.

#### The Five-Step Process of Developing an EFE Matrix
According to the strategic management framework, an EFE Matrix is developed in five steps:
1. List Key External Factors: Include a total of 15 to 20 factors, encompassing both opportunities and threats that affect the firm and its industry.
2. Assign Weights: Assign each factor a weight ranging from 0.0 (not important) to 1.0 (very important). The weight indicates the relative importance of that factor to being successful in the industry. The sum of all weights must equal 1.0.
3. Assign Ratings: Assign a rating between 1 and 4 to each factor to indicate how effectively the firm's current strategies respond to the factor:
   * 4 = The response is superior.
   * 3 = The response is above average.
   * 2 = The response is average.
   * 1 = The response is poor..
4. Multiply Weight by Rating: Determine a weighted score for each variable.
5. Sum Weighted Scores: Determine the total weighted score for the organization. The total possible score ranges from 1.0 to 4.0, with 2.5 being the average.

#### 1. Weight

By assigning these weights, the strategist acknowledges that while all KSFs matter, visibility and operational scale are currently the most decisive factors for attaining superior returns.

#### 2. Ratings
Ratings in a CPM represent how effectively a firm responds to each success factor, ranging from 4 (major strength) to 1 (major weakness). For Cosco, the ratings reveal a company defined by high-quality intangible assets but crippled by tangible operational inefficiencies. These ratings provide a clear mandate for management: they must protect their "Rating 4" design expertise while aggressively addressing the "Rating 1" cost failures that threaten the firm's survival.

#### 3. Score and Total Score
The Weighted Score for each factor is the mathematical product of the Weight multiplied by the Rating. This calculation allows strategists to see exactly how much each factor contributes to the firm's overall competitive power. For instance, Cosco's Brand Reputation contributes a high score of 0.52 (0.13 weight x 4 rating), while its Low Cost Structure contributes a negligible 0.05 (0.05 weight x 1 rating). This contrast illustrates that while Cosco is a powerhouse in terms of identity, its financial foundation is exceptionally thin.

The Total Weighted Score is the sum of all individual scores, providing a numerical snapshot of a firm's competitive standing. Cosco receives a Total Weighted Score of 2.64. In the strategic management framework, scores range from 1.0 (poor) to 4.0 (superior), with a score of 2.50 representing the average industry performer. Cosco's score of 2.64 indicates that the company is currently performing slightly above the average. However, this total score masks a dangerous internal tension; the company's strengths in leadership and design are barely outweighing its significant financial weaknesses. While a 2.64 keeps Cosco in the game, it lacks the superior score (closer to 4.0) that would indicate the firm is effectively exploiting its environment to generate sustainable profits rather than just revenue.

#### 4. Analyzing the Score
Analyzing the score of 2.64 requires looking beyond the number to the why of the performance. Because Cosco's score is above 2.50, it indicates that the firm's strategies are generally effective at responding to external opportunities, such as public procurement demand. The company is doing an above-average job of managing social trends, such as the rise of ergonomics and biophilic design, which allow it to maintain its National Custom Partner status.

However, the score analysis reveals a firm on financial life support. The low scores in cost-related areas indicate that Cosco is inefficient at turning its manufacturing resources into bottom-line profit. The PHP 29.5 million cumulative deficit and the PHP 17.3 million inventory bottleneck are the primary anchors dragging down the total score. Even though sales grew by 24% in 2025, the failure to translate this into a higher score suggests that the firm's current market penetration strategy is not protecting its margins. An analysis of a 2.64 score suggests a company with high customer value but low operational integrity. For this score to improve, Cosco must move from being a design-led firm to an efficiency-led firm, prioritizing production automation to fix the low-scoring areas in labor productivity and cost control.

#### 5. Compare the Score
The final step in CPM analysis is to compare the scores between the sample firm and its rivals, as the firm with the highest total score is considered the strongest. When placed against its primary competitors, Cosco is revealed as the weakest player among the industry's major strategic groups.

* The Global Design Leader (3.53): This competitor is the industry's strongest player. They possess Rating 4 strengths in almost every category, particularly in Superior IT/Automation and Strong Online Presence, allowing them to dominate the high-end export market that Cosco aspires to enter.
* The Volume Wholesaler (3.30): This rival excels in operational efficiency. Their Rating 4 in Low Cost Structure, Market Share, and Sales per Employee shows they leverage economies of scale that Cosco currently lacks.
* Cosco (2.64): While Cosco meets the industry average, it trails the Volume Wholesaler by 0.66 points and the Global Design Leader by 0.89 points.

This comparison identifies a massive efficiency gap. While Cosco can compete with the Global Design Leader on customization (both Rating 4), it cannot compete with either rival on price or productivity. The comparison proves that Cosco's niche focus on government relations is a necessary defensive move, but without adopting the means for achieving strategy, such as automation or joint ventures, it remains highly vulnerable to being out-scaled or under-priced by more efficient rivals.

## C. Matching Stage
The Matching Stage serves as the vital analytical bridge in the strategy-formulation framework for Cosco Integrated Office Systems, Inc., transitioning from the quantified audits of the Input Stage to the generation of feasible alternative strategies by pairing internal resources with external environmental trends. For a firm currently navigating a PHP 29.5 million cumulative deficit while simultaneously experiencing a 24% increase in sales, this stage is essential for converting market momentum into long-term financial stability. By utilizing specialized tools including the TOWS, SPACE, BCG, IE, and Grand Strategy Matrices, Cosco can develop proactive maneuvers that align its internal building blocks with what the market allows it to do. For example, a Strengths-Opportunities (SO) strategy would match Cosco's 20 years of bespoke design expertise with massive public demand, such as the PHP 72.05 million SSS furniture procurement plan, to secure high-value revenue that volume-driven wholesalers cannot easily access. Simultaneously, a Weaknesses-Opportunities (WO) strategy pairs the firm's 92% cost-of-sales ratio with the emerging production automation trend (2026-2032), recommending an investment in robotic assembly to lean out operations and reverse its PHP 5.58 million net loss. Furthermore, Strengths-Threats (ST) strategies leverage the powerhouse leadership of COO Linda V. Dela Cruz to navigate intense rivalry from 250+ brands by focusing on inimitable biophilic and wellness designs that avoid competitive parity. Finally, Weaknesses-Threats (WT) strategies might involve defensive retrenchment or liquidating the PHP 17.3 million inventory bottleneck to improve liquidity during periods of high inflation. Ultimately, the Matching Stage ensures Cosco's future strategic path is grounded in an objective alignment of its unique design capabilities with dynamic market shifts, moving the firm from financial life support toward a sustainable competitive advantage.
