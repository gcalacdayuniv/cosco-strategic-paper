# Chapter 5: Strategies in Action

## A. Introduction
Strategies represent the specific, planned actions an organization takes to achieve its long-term goals. For Cosco Integrated Office Systems, Inc., the transition from analyzing its environment to implementing these strategies is the most critical phase for its future survival. According to the reference material, a firm that continues to employ a previously successful strategy may eventually fall victim to a competitor if it does not adapt. Cosco's current situation is a prime example of this need for strategic movement; while the company has a strong 20-year history and has seen sales grow to P28.7 million in 2025, it is simultaneously grappling with a net loss of P5.58 million and a cumulative deficit of P29.5 million.

Strategy is essentially a problem of allocating limited resources against a decisive opportunity. For Cosco, this means leveraging its core competency in bespoke office design and its extensive P17.3 million raw material inventory to capture high-value market segments. This chapter identifies the strategic paths-including intensive, integration, and generic models-that Cosco can pursue to bridge the gap between its current financial distress and its vision of "Innovating the Future." By adopting a proactive rather than reactive stance, management can ensure the company does not just float pointlessly but moves toward a sustainable competitive advantage in the Philippine furniture industry.

## B. Long Term Objectives
Long-term objectives represent the specific, quantifiable results that Cosco seeks to achieve over the next two to five years, serving as a clear standard for evaluating managerial performance and guiding executive decisions. For a company currently navigating a severe structural capital deficiency and a demanding economic landscape, these mandatory goals must heavily focus on achieving rapid financial stabilization and sweeping operational efficiency to ensure organizational survival and sustained viability. To spearhead this critical financial recovery, Cosco aims to execute a complete, undeniable turnaround from its current debilitating net loss position to a sustainable, positive net income of at least P3 million by the end of the 2027 fiscal year. This necessary paradigm shift involves a targeted, uncompromising strategic focus on drastically reducing the high cost of sales, an area of profound inefficiency that currently consumes a problematic 92% of the company's total revenue and severely restricts capital accumulation. Concurrently, the organization is pursuing aggressive revenue expansion, setting a firm objective to systematically increase its baseline annual sales revenue from the current stagnant level of P28.7 million to a vastly improved, highly ambitious target of P45 million by the year 2029. This essential top-line growth will be primarily driven by a dedicated corporate initiative to consistently secure at least two major, high-value institutional government contracts annually, specifically targeting lucrative agreements that are substantially similar in scale to the P72 million Social Security System furniture procurement plan previously identified within the 2025 market data. Furthermore, effective debt and liquidity management remains absolutely vital; consequently, Cosco strictly plans to strategically reduce its total outstanding liabilities, with a particular emphasis on aggressively paying down the burdensome P32.1 million in existing trade payables, aiming for a 25% reduction over the next three consecutive years. This critical deleveraging process will be directly facilitated by significantly improving the inventory turnover rate of its massive raw material stockpile. Finally, addressing operational modernization, the company firmly intends to resolutely transition from outdated manual manufacturing processes to comprehensive production automation by 2028, definitively lowering labor costs, which were reduced to P2.6 million in 2025 but still impact overall profit margins.

## C. Integration Strategies
Integration strategies allow Cosco Integrated Office Systems, Inc. to gain greater control over its distributors, suppliers, or competitors. In the context of the Philippine furniture industry, which is currently facing high production costs and a flat growth export market, these strategies are essential for protecting profit margins and stabilizing the company's financial position.

### Forward Integration
Forward integration involves a manufacturer gaining ownership or increased control over its distributors or retailers. Currently, Cosco engages in both manufacturing and trading on a wholesale and retail basis. To further this strategy, Cosco can focus on Direct Marketing by enhancing its interactive digital presence. By using its corporate profile to sell directly to government agencies and private corporations, the company can bypass intermediaries, thereby capturing a larger share of the profit margin and gaining better control over final pricing.

### Backward Integration
Backward integration is the process of seeking ownership or increased control of a firm's suppliers. This is a particularly effective strategy for Cosco because its cost of sales consumed 92% of its revenue in 2025. The company already holds a significant raw material inventory of P17.3 million. By entering into exclusive contracts or acquiring local wood and metal processing firms, Cosco can protect itself against the global inflation and U.S. tariffs that threaten to drive up the cost of essential inputs like hardwood. Locking in stable prices for materials is a "sensible enterprise" when current suppliers are costly or undependable.

### Horizontal Integration
Horizontal integration refers to seeking ownership or control over competitors. With over 250 brands competing in the local market, the industry is highly fragmented. Cosco could pursue horizontal integration by merging with or acquiring smaller specialty design shops in the Cavite or Metro Manila area. This would allow the firm to eliminate rivals, achieve economies of scale, and acquire new talent or unique biophilic design capabilities that are currently in high social demand.

### Strategic Rationale for the Expansion
* **A. Financial Protection (Backward):** The most critical move for Cosco is backward integration. Because the company's cost of sales currently consumes 92% of its revenue, the guideline regarding stable prices is the most decisive factor for its recovery. Locking in supply costs is the fastest way to protect the company's razor-thin gross profit.
* **B. Capturing Value (Forward):** With the Social Security System (SSS) earmarking over 72 million pesos for furniture in 2025, Cosco has a decisive opportunity to act as its own distributor. By following the forward integration guideline of handling the business of a distributor, the firm can keep the full profit margin of these massive government contracts rather than sharing them with intermediaries.
* **C. Scaling for Automation (Horizontal):** To move toward the industry trend of production automation (2026-2032), Cosco needs volume. Horizontal integration is effective here because it allows the company to reach the economies of scale necessary to justify investing in robotic assembly and CNC cutting tools, which currently have a low net carrying value in their assets.

## D. Intensive Strategies
Intensive strategies are fundamentally deployed when an organization actively seeks to significantly improve its competitive market position utilizing its existing portfolio of products or services. For Cosco Integrated Office Systems, Inc., the immediate implementation of these aggressive, focused strategies is absolutely essential to rapidly reverse recent financial losses and stabilize the corporate bottom line. By strategically leveraging its firmly established, twenty-year industry reputation for excellence and its highly specialized bespoke design capabilities, Cosco can deeply penetrate existing markets and attract a broader clientele. This approach demands a rigorous focus on market penetration, aiming to capture a larger market share through intensified marketing initiatives, competitive pricing models, and enhanced customer relationship management. Furthermore, the company must proactively engage in targeted product development, refining its current office system offerings to better meet the evolving ergonomic and technological demands of modern workspaces. By capitalizing on its proven historical track record and deep institutional knowledge, Cosco can effectively upsell and cross-sell customized solutions to its loyal client base while simultaneously outmaneuvering newer competitors. Ultimately, executing these intensive strategies will allow the organization to maximize the latent value of its core competencies, driving sustainable revenue growth and restoring robust, long-term financial health moving confidently forward.

### Market Penetration
For Cosco, market penetration involves securing a larger share of the Philippine office furniture market without changing its core product line of modular systems and desks.
* **The SSS Opportunity:** The most decisive application of this strategy is targeting the Social Security System (SSS) 2025 Annual Procurement Plan. The SSS has allocated over P72.05 million for furniture, P22.73 million for modular e-center furniture, and P6.88 million for treasury division furniture. By intensifying its bidding efforts for these specific, high-value government contracts, Cosco can increase its sales volume (aiming for a target of P45 million by 2029) without the risk of entering unknown markets.
* **Inventory Utilization:** With P17.32 million in raw material inventory on hand, Cosco is already staged to fulfill these massive orders, allowing them to use price adjustments as a tactic to win bids against competitors while still clearing their stockpile.

### Market Development
For Cosco, market development means taking its established bespoke office design capability and applying it outside of its primary Metro Manila and Bacoor, Cavite service area.
* **Geographic Expansion:** Industry trends indicate that Cebu and Davao are becoming high-growth hubs for custom office furniture due to the rise of hybrid work and new urban developments. Moving its services into these provincial business hubs constitutes geographic market development.
* **New Market Segments:** While Cosco currently focuses heavily on large-scale institutional and government clients (like the SSS), it can apply market development by targeting the growing compact office and hybrid work segment. Small businesses in the Philippines are increasingly looking for wall-mounted desks and foldable tables for limited square footage. By marketing its existing manufacturing skills to these small-office owners, Cosco enters a new set of markets.

### Product Development
For Cosco, product development is the commercial engine behind its slogan, "Innovating the Future". In a market crowded with over 250 brands, the company cannot rely solely on standard catalog furniture. Instead, it must leverage its core competency in customized manufacturing to create exceptional and different offerings that justify premium pricing and offset its P26.5 million cost of sales.
* **Wellness and Ergonomics:** Modifying existing desk and chair designs to include adjustable height features and enhanced lumbar support to address the rising demand for employee well-being.
* **Tech-Integration:** Updating its modular office systems to include built-in wireless charging pads, integrated AV setups for virtual meetings, and advanced cable management systems.
* **Sustainability:** Transitioning from traditional materials to eco-friendly alternatives such as bamboo, reclaimed wood, or recycled metal to capture the growing segment of environmentally conscious corporate clients.

## E. Diversification Strategies
For Cosco Integrated Office Systems, Inc., diversification strategies represent a strategic move to add new products or services to its portfolio, particularly when its core furniture manufacturing industry faces flat growth or intense rivalry. These strategies are categorized into two primary types: related and unrelated diversification.

### Related Diversification Strategy
This strategy involves moving into new industries that possess competitively valuable cross-business strategic fits with Cosco's existing value chain. For Cosco, this means leveraging its 20 years of niche design know-how and its manufacturing capabilities in Bacoor, Cavite. The goal is to transfer expertise or combine activities to attain lower costs and capitalize on the existing corporate brand.
* **Sustainable Material Production:** Utilizing current R&D into biophilic materials to manufacture related products like eco-friendly acoustic panels or flooring made from reclaimed wood and bamboo.
* **Workplace Wellness Consulting:** Moving from selling physical ergonomic furniture to providing professional certification and assessment services for office wellness, leveraging its deep understanding of ergonomics and employee productivity.

### Unrelated Diversification Strategy
This strategy focuses on capitalizing on a portfolio of businesses that can deliver a high return on investment, regardless of whether they have a strategic fit with Cosco's furniture manufacturing core. This approach favors firms that have the capital and managerial talent (often secured through stockholder advances in Cosco's case) to compete in entirely new industries where profit prospects are higher.
* **Commercial Facility Management:** Leveraging its strong institutional relations with high-value clients like the Social Security System (SSS) to offer broader office management services that go beyond furniture maintenance.
* **Industrial Logistics:** Repurposing its outbound logistics network or its principal headquarters' space in Cavite to provide warehousing and storage services for other local industries.

## F. Defensive Strategies
For Cosco Integrated Office Systems, Inc., defensive strategies are management tools used to reorganize operations or liquidate assets to address financial instability and fending off competitive pressure. Despite a 24% increase in sales in 2025, the company ended the year with a net loss of P5.58 million and a cumulative deficit of P29.5 million, making these strategies highly relevant for its survival.

### Retrenchment
This involves reducing assets and costs to reverse effects of declining profits and sales. For Cosco, this is the most critical strategy to address a cost of sales that consumes 92% of its revenue. Specific actions would include trimming the P7.7 million in general administrative expenses and leaning out production to better utilize its P17.3 million raw material inventory, which currently ties up too much working capital.

### Divestiture
This strategy involves selling a division or part of the organization to raise capital for further strategic investment or to focus on core businesses. Cosco could consider selling off non-core assets or non-performing trading divisions to raise the cash needed to settle its P32.1 million in trade payables.

### Liquidation
This is the recognition of defeat where all of a company's assets are sold in parts for their tangible worth. Given that Cosco is currently on financial life support through P15.4 million in stockholder advances, liquidation would be a last resort to minimize losses for the Bantiling family if profitability cannot be restored.

1. Type 1: Cost Leadership - Low Cost
A low-cost leadership strategy targets a broad market by offering
products at the absolute lowest price available. For Cosco, this strategy is
currently a significant challenge. The firm operates with a small workforce
of 26 registered employees and faces intense rivalry from over 250 local
and global brands, many of whom possess greater economies of scale.
To effectively pursue Type 1 Cost Leadership, Cosco would need to
undergo a massive internal reorganization. Sources suggest that the firm
must invest in production automation, such as CNC cutting and robotic
assembly, to drastically reduce the labor and material waste that currently
keeps its production costs at P26.5 million against sales of P28.7 million.
Without these efficiencies, attempting to compete as the lowest-cost
provider in the broad market would likely deepen its existing P29.5 million
capital deficiency.

2. Type 2: Cost Leadership - Best Value
The best-value strategy aims to provide a broad range of
customers with products at the best price-value ratio on the market. This
approach is more realistic for Cosco than pure low-cost leadership. The
company currently holds a massive raw material inventory valued at P17.3
million. While this inventory is currently a bottleneck that ties up idle
capital, it also represents a strategic resource.
By leveraging this stockpile, Cosco can fulfill large-scale orders
without the price volatility or delays associated with just-in-time sourcing
from international suppliers. If Cosco can lean out its operations and
improve its Total Assets Turnover, it could offer high-quality modular office
systems at a price point that undercuts high-end international design
houses while offering better durability than mass-produced
Ready-to-Assemble (RTA) retail alternatives.

3. Type 3: Differentiation
Differentiation involves creating unique products that are perceived
as extraordinary and inimitable, allowing the firm to charge a premium.
This is Cosco's current core competency. As a National Custom Partner,
Cosco focuses on bespoke office design, tailoring environments to the
specific brand identities of its clients.
Cosco's R&D vision, "Innovating the Future," centers on
high-growth trends such as biophilic design (integrating reclaimed wood
and bamboo) and workplace wellness/ergonomics. Because these custom
systems are difficult for rivals to copy and serve as a direct extension of a
client's corporate culture, Cosco can command higher margins from
price-insensitive institutional buyers. The 20 years of niche design
know-how held by leadership, specifically COO Linda V. Dela Cruz, is a
crown jewel that supports this differentiation strategy.

4. Type 4: Low-Cost Focus
This strategy targets a narrow niche group with basic, no-frills
products at the lowest possible price. For Cosco, this could involve
creating a specialized "Essentials" line for local startups and small
businesses in the Cavite and Metro Manila hubs.
By limiting the scope to a specific geographic area and a restricted
catalog of standard desks and chairs, Cosco could minimize its P7.7
million in general administrative overhead and distribution costs. This
would allow the firm to maintain a steady, high-velocity revenue stream
from small-volume buyers who do not require the complex bespoke
services Cosco provides to its larger clients.

5. Type 5: Best-Value Focus (Focused Differentiation)
A best-value focus strategy targets a niche by satisfying their
specific tastes better than broad-market rivals. Cosco is already highly
effective in this category through its government institutional relations.
The prime example is the SSS 2025 procurement plan, which
includes over P72 million for furniture. This niche requires strict legal
compliance, specific design standards, and a level of trust that global
wholesalers cannot easily match. By focusing its resources on these
high-value government contracts, Cosco uses its sustainable competitive
advantage in compliance and specialized service to secure large, reliable
revenue streams that broad-market competitors find too costly or difficult
to enter.

Strategic Conclusion
For Cosco to reverse its net loss, it must move away from a broad,
cost-heavy manufacturing model and lean into a Best-Value Focus (Type
5). By combining its differentiation expertise in bespoke design with a
rigorous push toward production automation, Cosco can protect its
razor-thin margins and ensure that its robust sales growth finally translates
into bottom-line profitability.

H. Achieving Strategies
For Cosco Integrated Office Systems, Inc. (Cosco), achieving a
sustainable competitive advantage requires more than just internal focus; it
necessitates leveraging specific strategic means to navigate a hyper-competitive
Philippine furniture market. While the company has seen 24% sales growth in
2025, it remains in a precarious financial position with a cumulative deficit of
P29.5 million and a cost of sales that consumes 92% of revenue. According to
strategic management principles, firms can pursue their objectives through
various means such as cooperation, joint ventures, mergers, first-mover
advantages, and outsourcing.

Cooperation Among Competitors
Cooperation is a strategy where competing companies unite to gain
common benefits that they could not achieve alone. For Cosco, this is
increasingly necessary because the furniture industry is becoming too
complex to survive in isolation.
Cosco can cooperate with other local manufacturers to lobby
against international trade and tariff pressures. Industry leaders in the
Philippines have already begun collective appeals for diplomatic
engagement to retain zero tariffs on exports and to increase US hardwood
imports as a reciprocal trade gesture.
By forming alliances with rivals, Cosco could share research or
manufacturing capacity for expensive biophilic materials like reclaimed
wood and bamboo, which are currently in high demand but costly to
process individually. This co-opetition allows Cosco to reduce the burden
of high raw material procurement, which currently stands at P15 million
annually.

Joint Venture and Partnering
A joint venture occurs when two or more companies form a
temporary partnership or a new entity to share equity ownership, risks,
and rewards. This is often less risky for companies than a full merger.
To move from a "National Custom Partner" to a "Global Design
Leader," Cosco can seek foreign partnerships. Strategic partners can
bring Cosco's craftsmanship to international markets like the US, Europe,
or Hong Kong.
Cosco's R&D vision, "Innovating the Future," could be realized by
partnering with tech firms to develop smart office furniture. By combining
its design know-how with a partner's expertise in built-in digital
capabilities, Cosco can create extraordinary and inimitable products that
are difficult for rivals to duplicate.
Cosco must ensure that its joint venture managers work together
daily and that the venture provides clear advantages to both parties to
avoid the common causes of alliance failure.

Merger and Acquisition
A merger happens when two equally sized companies join to form
one organization, while an acquisition occurs when a larger firm buys a
smaller one.
Given its current net loss of P5.58 million, Cosco is not in a strong
position to acquire large rivals. However, it could be a candidate for a
"White Knight" acquisition where a more financially stable firm acquires
Cosco to save it from its P29.5 million capital deficiency while preserving
its 20 years of niche design know-how.
Alternatively, Cosco could use a backward integration strategy to
acquire a small local supplier of bamboo or reclaimed wood. This would
give Cosco more control over its supply chain and help stabilize its P17.3
million raw material inventory costs, which are currently vulnerable to
supplier leverage.

First-Mover Advantage
A first-mover advantage is the lead a company gains by being the
first to enter a new market or develop a new product. For a strategist like
Linda V. Dela Cruz, being a first mover can build a powerful brand
reputation and form strongly loyal customers.
Cosco has a window of opportunity to be a first mover in the
widespread adoption of furniture production automation in the Philippines.
The market is expected to shift toward robotic assembly and CNC cutting
between 2026 and 2032. By investing early, Cosco can generate cost
advantages through precision and reduced material waste, addressing its
major weakness of high production costs.
By being the first to fully commit to a circular economy model, using
only 100% sustainable materials and non-toxic finishes, Cosco can
capture the growing green consumer base before its 250+ rivals catch up.
First movers face high risks of product failure or imitation by slow
movers who learn from the leader's mistakes. Cosco must be a fast
learner to sustain any lead it gains.

Outsourcing
Outsourcing involves subcontracting parts of a company's value
chain to other firms that specialize in those activities. This is often done to
lower costs or allow a company to focus on its core competencies.
Cosco's true sustainable advantage lies in its bespoke office design
and executive leadership. It currently struggles with high general and
administrative expenses (P7.7 million).
Cosco could outsource its outbound logistics and delivery to
third-party providers. This would allow the firm to focus its limited internal
resources on manufacturing and design rather than maintaining a complex
fleet for national distribution.
The company lacks an integrated ERP system to optimize
inventory. Outsourcing its Management Information System (MIS)
